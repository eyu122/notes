§ INTRODUCTIONWhy Foreign Policy Matters to You

Every day, forces shaped by foreign policy decisions made thousands of kilometers away affect your life — the price of fuel, the availability of technology, the safety of your borders, the terms of the loans funding the roads you drive on. Foreign policy is not an abstract game played by diplomats in marble halls; it is the mechanism through which states navigate a world that has no police, no global government, and no guarantee that anyone will come to your aid when you need them.

This unit asks three interlocking questions: What drives states to act as they do in international relations? (national interest); How do states translate those drives into coherent action? (foreign policy); and What tools do they use to pursue their goals short of war? (diplomacy and its instruments). We will then ground all of this theory in the concrete history of Ethiopian foreign policy — six regimes, six sets of challenges, and a remarkably consistent thread of core interests running through all of them.

Section 2.1

§ 2.1Defining National Interest

What Is National Interest?

The term national interest is simultaneously the most used and most abused concept in international relations. Politicians invoke it to justify wars, economic sanctions, humanitarian interventions, and covert operations. Critics claim it is a smokescreen for elite self-interest dressed in patriotic clothing. Academics argue over whether it can even be objectively defined. Yet for all its contested character, national interest remains the indispensable conceptual anchor of foreign policy analysis.

The Latin phrase raison d'état — "reason of the state" — captures the essence: national interest is the justification a state offers for its actions toward other states. It is the answer to the question: why is your government doing this? Three foundational scholarly definitions illuminate the concept from different angles.

Three Foundational Definitions

Holsti's aspirational definition: National interest is "an image of the future state of affairs and future set of conditions that governments, through individual policy makers, aspire to bring about by wielding influence abroad and by changing or sustaining the behaviors of other states." — Note how this centers the role of power as the instrument of realization.

Seabury's normative definition: In the normative sense, national interest refers to the "set of purposes which a nation should seek to realize in the conduct of its foreign relations" — what ought to be pursued. This has an ethical dimension absent from purely power-based definitions.

Seabury's descriptive definition: National interest may also be regarded as "those purposes which the nation through its leadership appears to pursue persistently over time" — what states actually do over time, stripped of official rhetoric. The analyst looks at behavior, not declarations.

The gulf between Seabury's normative and descriptive versions is analytically crucial. What a state says its national interest is (normative) and what its sustained behavior reveals it to actually be (descriptive) are frequently different things. States routinely frame self-serving policies in the language of universal values. Critical analysis requires watching behavior over time, not press releases.

The Science vs. Art Debate

Can national interest be scientifically determined — derived through rational, objective calculation of what maximizes the state's welfare? Or is it fundamentally an art — a subjective, value-laden, politically contested enterprise?

This debate traces back to Plato's philosopher-king ideal: the idea that a small group of wise, disinterested experts, trained to think in terms of the collective good, can make optimal policy decisions. Three assumptions underpin this view: (1) such wise individuals exist and can be identified; (2) they will not be corrupted by the enormous power they wield; (3) their decisions can be implemented by loyal, obedient bureaucracies.

Each assumption is problematic. Power corrupts; bureaucracies have their own interests; and "optimal" policy for whom? Plato's ideal has historically inspired authoritarian regimes — the claim that the leader alone knows the national interest and therefore needs no democratic accountability. The alternative — democratic deliberation — is messier and slower but subjects national interest definitions to public scrutiny.

The Eight Criteria for Defining National Interest

Columbis identified a range of criteria that simultaneously compete in shaping how governments define national interest. These are not theoretical ideals — they are real forces operating inside every foreign policy bureaucracy. Understanding them explains why foreign policy is rarely a clean, rational calculation of "the national good" and more often a product of competing institutional pressures, ideological commitments, personal ambitions, and historical path dependencies.

01
Operational Philosophy

Two styles: synoptic (bold, sweeping — assume enough information for major policy change) vs. incremental (cautious, trial-and-error — build on existing policy). Leadership personality and political circumstances determine which style dominates.

02
Ideological Criteria

Governments define friends and enemies through ideological lenses. During the Cold War, communist vs. capitalist ideology determined alliances. Today, liberal democracy vs. authoritarianism shapes similar alignments.

03
Moral & Legal Criteria

Acting morally = keeping promises, respecting others' interests, avoiding exploitation. Acting legally = abiding by international law and treaty obligations. Many foreign policies are justified on moral and legal grounds — even when the real motives are strategic.

04
Pragmatic Criteria

Low-key, matter-of-fact, cost-benefit analysis. No moral sentimentality — act on scientific assessment of what will benefit the state. Morgenthau's preferred standard for realists. "Does it work?" replaces "Is it right?"

05
Professional Advancement

Officials in large bureaucracies may shape policy to serve their own career interests — "go along to get along." Conformist behavior resists new thinking. This criterion explains bureaucratic inertia: policy persists not because it works but because change threatens those who benefit from existing arrangements.

06
Partisan Criteria

Equating the survival of a political party, ethnic group, or religious community with national survival. Budget battles among government agencies are a classic example: each agency defines its institutional interest as the national interest. Military budgets, foreign office resources — all reflect partisan competition.

07
Ethnic/Racial Criteria

States may act to protect co-ethnic or co-religious populations abroad. Hungary's protection of ethnic Hungarians in neighboring states; Turkey's assertive policies regarding Turkic communities in Central Asia; Ethiopia's policies toward the Somali region.

08
Foreign Dependency Criteria

Post-colonial states often maintain ties of economic and security dependence on former colonial powers, limiting their foreign policy autonomy. Governments dependent on foreign aid for survival may compromise long-term national interests for immediate external support — defining national interest in terms that serve their patrons.

Analytical Takeaway

The multiplicity of these criteria explains one of the most important observations in foreign policy analysis: national interest is not a single, objectively determinable thing. It is a product of competing wills, ambitions, motivations, and demands — domestic as much as external. When a government announces its "national interest," ask: which criteria dominated this definition? Who was in the room? Whose interests are served? Whose are excluded?

This skeptical lens does not mean national interest is meaningless — it means we should analyze it critically rather than accept official formulations at face value.

Realist vs. Idealist Interpretations

The most fundamental theoretical divide in defining national interest runs between the realist and idealist schools of international relations. Both agree that states pursue national interest. They disagree profoundly on what national interest consists of and how it should be defined.

Realist View — Hans Morgenthau

National interest must be defined exclusively in terms of power — the ability to control or influence others' behavior, diplomatically or through coercion. In an anarchical international system (no global government, no guarantee of protection), survival and security are the primary interests of every state. Moral, legal, and ideological considerations are dangerous distractions that produce miscalculation and catastrophe. The good diplomat — the rational diplomat — is a prudent diplomat: one who carefully balances aspirations against capabilities, never overreaching. National interest must be proportional to national power.

Idealist View — The Liberal Response

Idealists reject the realist separation of power from morality and law. Even the most self-interested foreign policies — declaring war, forming alliances, providing foreign aid, humanitarian intervention — are always justified on moral and legal grounds. These justifications are not mere window-dressing; they shape what policies are feasible and legitimate in international society. Furthermore, realism's exclusive focus on state interest makes it blind to cross-cutting global problems — climate change, poverty, pandemics, nuclear proliferation — that require collective, values-based international action no single state's self-interest can produce.

"The politician… asks: 'How does this policy affect the power of my country?' And we must ask the same question." — Hans Morgenthau, Politics Among Nations (1948)
Critical Synthesis

Neither pure realism nor pure idealism fully captures the reality of foreign policy. Morgenthau himself acknowledged that prudence — the virtue he most valued — requires judgment about when moral considerations should constrain purely power-based calculations. And idealists who ignore power dynamics produce policies that are morally admirable but strategically futile. The best foreign policy analysis holds both lenses simultaneously: asking both "what is this state capable of?" and "what values should constrain how it uses that capability?"

Ethiopia's post-1991 foreign policy doctrine explicitly attempts this synthesis: it frames democracy and development as strategic national interests — not merely moral commitments — because a democratic, economically developed Ethiopia is also a more secure, less vulnerable Ethiopia.

Section 2.2

§ 2.2Understanding Foreign Policy

2.2.1 — Defining Foreign Policy

If national interest is the destination, foreign policy is the roadmap and vehicle. Foreign policy translates a state's interests — its ambitions, fears, and values in international relations — into concrete decisions, strategies, and actions directed at other states and international institutions.

Foreign Policy — Three Definitions

Rochester's definition: Foreign policy refers to "the set of priorities and percepts established by national leaders to serve as guidelines for choosing among various courses of action in specific situations in international affairs." — This emphasizes the decision-making framework aspect.

Operational definition: Foreign policy involves the general purposes, specific strategies, and concrete instruments — diplomatic, economic, and military — that states employ to achieve or promote their national interests in the international arena.

Morgenthau's minimal baseline: Whatever else it aspires to, the minimum foreign policy objective of any state is survival — the preservation of physical integrity (territory), political identity (the system of government), and cultural identity (the people's ethnic, linguistic, and historical character).

Key Distinction — Foreign Policy vs. Diplomacy

Foreign policy is the set of goals, strategies, and decisions a state adopts to guide its external relations. It includes every instrument — war, sanctions, aid, propaganda, diplomacy.

Diplomacy is one specific instrument of foreign policy — the conduct of state-to-state relations through negotiation, dialogue, and representation, without resort to force. To interact without diplomacy would limit a state's foreign policy to conflict or espionage. Diplomacy is therefore the essential peacetime machinery of foreign policy implementation.

Analogy: foreign policy is the strategy; diplomacy is the primary tactic for pursuing it peacefully.

2.2.2 — Foreign Policy Objectives: Short, Medium & Long Range

Holsti classifies foreign policy objectives along a time dimension, based on three criteria: (1) the value placed on the objective — how central is it to national survival and identity? (2) the time element — when must it be achieved? (3) the demands it imposes on other states — how much conflict will its pursuit generate?

① Short Range — Core Values

The irreducible minimum — what a state will fight and die for. Includes: territorial integrity, political independence, preservation of the governing system, and cultural survival.

These are "sacrosanct" — article of faith that entire peoples accept without questioning. No amount of diplomatic or economic pressure from outside changes them. They are short-range because all other goals are meaningless if the state itself ceases to exist.

Example: For Ethiopia, territorial integrity has been a core value across every regime — from Tewodros through the EPRDF. The Adwa victory (1896) is the defining expression of this.

② Middle Range — Welfare & Development

Varies dramatically by a state's development level, capabilities, and ideology. The common baseline: economic betterment — meeting domestic welfare expectations through international action.

No state is self-sufficient. Interdependence means satisfying domestic economic needs requires external engagement: trade, foreign aid, access to markets and technology, investment. Governments that fail to deliver economic improvement face political defeat.

Example: Ethiopia's post-1991 "economic diplomacy" — attracting FDI, seeking export markets, accessing concessional loans — is a medium-range objective pursuing domestic poverty reduction through external engagement.

③ Long Range — Systemic Vision

Plans, dreams, and visions concerning the ultimate political or ideological organization of the international system. Universal in scope — not demands against particular states but a blueprint for reordering the entire global order.

Only great powers have the capabilities to seriously pursue these. But smaller states also articulate long-range visions — they serve a domestic mobilizing function even if their international impact is limited. Critical caveat: visions without capabilities remain rhetoric.

Example: The Derg's vision of international socialism; the EPRDF's vision of African-led developmental democracy as an alternative to Western liberal conditionality.

2.2.3 — Foreign Policy Behavior: Wolfers' Three Patterns

Arnold Wolfers, one of the 20th century's leading IR scholars, argued that despite the bewildering diversity of state behavior, all foreign policy ultimately reduces to three fundamental patterns. Every foreign policy decision fits within one or a combination of these. The framework is simple, but its application is analytically powerful.

▲

Self-Preservation

Maintaining the status quo. The state seeks to preserve the existing international order because that order benefits it. Classic example: United States post-1945. American power shaped the Bretton Woods institutions (IMF, World Bank, GATT/WTO), the UN Security Council structure, and the liberal international order. Having designed the system, America defends it vigorously — any reform that dilutes American structural advantages is resisted through diplomatic pressure, sanctions, or force if necessary.

◆

Self-Extension

Revising the status quo in one's own favor. Rising powers seek to restructure international institutions and rules to better reflect their capabilities and interests. Example: China, India, Brazil (BRICS) — all challenging Western-designed international financial and political institutions. China's Belt and Road Initiative, the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank, and assertive territorial claims in the South China Sea are all self-extension moves designed to expand Chinese influence in a system it did not design.

▼

Self-Abnegation

Revising the status quo in someone else's favor. Weak states that fail to defend their own interests and make concessions to stronger powers. The pattern of many Least Developed Countries (LDCs) — dependent on foreign aid, vulnerable to conditionality, unable to define an autonomous foreign policy. Sacrificing long-term national interest for immediate benefits (aid disbursements, debt relief) that strengthen the donor's leverage. This is the structural consequence of the foreign-dependency criterion in defining national interest.

Critical Application — Ethiopia Across Wolfers' Framework

Ethiopia's foreign policy behavior has oscillated across all three patterns depending on the era. Under Haile Selassie's alignment with the US-led Western order: partially self-preservation. Under the Derg's challenge to Western capitalist structures: partly self-extension (within a socialist alternative order). Under post-1991 "inside-out" strategy: resisting self-abnegation through deliberate prioritization of domestic capability-building over foreign-aid dependency — trying to escape the LDC structural trap.

2.2.4 — Foreign Policy Dimensions: Alignment, Scope & Modus Operandi

Beyond Wolfers' three behavioral patterns, foreign policy can be analysed along three specific dimensions that describe how states position themselves and operate in the international system. These dimensions are not static — they shift with leadership changes, capability shifts, and evolving international circumstances.

Dimension 1 — Alignment

Alignment describes whether and with whom a state formally associates itself in international affairs. It is one of the most consequential foreign policy decisions: the choice of ally defines friends and enemies, triggers obligations, and can drag a state into conflicts it did not choose.

Alliance
Formal legal agreements for mutual military assistance. Benefits: pooled military resources, access to foreign bases, deterrence through collective commitment. Risks: entrapment (being dragged into an ally's conflicts); loss of autonomy; obligation to defend states whose interests may diverge from your own over time. Ethiopia's US alliance under Haile Selassie (1953–1974) is a textbook alliance — base rights in exchange for military aid and security guarantee.
Neutrality
Formal non-partisanship — refusal to align with any bloc. Advantages: avoids generating enemies; maintains flexibility; preserves good relations with all sides. Risk: no security guarantee; if war comes, no committed protector. Switzerland is the extreme case — refused UN membership until 2002. Austria, Sweden, and Finland maintained neutrality throughout the Cold War (though the last two have since joined NATO).
Non-Alignment
The characteristic Cold War pattern of developing countries — refusing formal alignment with either the Western (NATO) or Eastern (Warsaw Pact) blocs, pursuing development and sovereignty on an independent basis. The Non-Aligned Movement (NAM), founded in 1961 (Belgrade), gave this a collective institutional form. Ethiopia was a founding member. Practical limitation: in a bipolar world, true non-alignment was nearly impossible — both superpowers applied pressure to recruit developing countries into their camps.

Dimension 2 — Scope

Scope refers to the geographic reach of a state's international engagement. It is directly determined by national power and capabilities — states with greater economic and military resources can project influence further.

Dimension 3 — Modus Operandi

The mode of operation describes how a state pursues its goals — primarily through collective international mechanisms or primarily through its own independent action.

Multilateralism

Seeking solutions through diplomatic forums involving multiple states — UN, AU, WTO, regional organizations. Preferred by developing countries: multilateral forums amplify collective bargaining power against stronger states. Also preferred by principle-oriented states like Germany, which uses international institutions as both a strategic tool and a normative commitment to rules-based order. Multilateralism increases legitimacy but sacrifices speed and unilateral control of outcomes.

Unilateralism

Acting independently — using one's own economic and military muscle to impose outcomes. Reserved for powerful states with the capability to back their actions. The "carrot and stick" approach — using rewards and threats to reshape others' behavior without multilateral constraint. More controllable and faster than multilateralism, but lacks legitimacy and generates resistance. Classic US, Chinese, and Russian behavior on issues they consider non-negotiable.

2.2.5 — The Instruments of Foreign Policy

States pursue foreign policy objectives through a spectrum of instruments, ranging from diplomatic dialogue at the peaceable end to military force at the coercive extreme. The choice of instrument depends on the objective pursued, the state's capabilities, the relationship with the target, and the international normative environment. Most sophisticated foreign policies use combinations of instruments simultaneously.

① Diplomacy — The Primary Instrument

Diplomacy is the system of structured communication between states. Its central mechanism is bargaining — the exchange of proposals between parties with conflicting priorities to reach mutually acceptable solutions. Bargaining always involves power, but it substitutes the costs of conflict with the costs of concession. Effective diplomacy requires genuine incompatibility of positions (if there is total agreement, there is nothing to bargain) and some minimum level of willingness to find a resolution.

The evolution of diplomacy over the 20th century:

Old Diplomacy (Pre-WWI)

Bilateral, secret, ambassador-centered. Two ambassadors negotiating privately, in closed rooms, representing their sovereigns. Results announced as faits accomplis. No transparency; no public accountability. Produced the system of secret alliances that turned a Balkan assassination in 1914 into a world war within six weeks. President Wilson's "Fourteen Points" (1918) explicitly attacked secret diplomacy as a cause of war.

New Diplomacy (Post-WWI)

Multilateral: involving many states simultaneously in international forums (League of Nations, UN). Public: open negotiations, press coverage, civil society engagement. Summit-level: direct leader-to-leader diplomacy (US-Soviet summits, G7, G20, AU summits). Technical: specialist agencies handling health (WHO), trade (WTO), aviation (ICAO), environment (UNEP). Diplomacy is now a permanent, 24/7, multi-level enterprise, not an occasional event.

Rules of Effective Diplomacy

Be realistic: Match goals to capabilities. Overreach produces humiliation and strategic failure.

Be precise: Every word matters. Ambiguity in treaties creates crises later — as Ethiopia discovered with the Wuchalle Treaty's dual language problem (1889).

Seek common ground: Disputes begin negotiations; common ground ends them. Every successful negotiation involves concession.

Understand the other side: Appreciate opponents' constraints and perspectives even without agreeing with them. Empathy is a strategic tool, not a weakness.

Be patient: Anxiety produces unnecessary concessions and signals weakness. Diplomatic time horizons are longer than electoral cycles.

Leave avenues of retreat: Never trap your opponent in a corner. Even rats fight when cornered. Provide face-saving exits — this is not generosity, it is strategic wisdom.

② Economic Instruments

Economic interdependence creates both vulnerabilities and leverage. States exploit others' economic needs to influence behavior — rewarding compliance and punishing defiance through manipulation of trade flows, investment, and financial access. Holsti identifies three purposes of economic instruments: (1) achieving any foreign policy objective by exploiting economic dependence; (2) increasing or depleting an adversary's capabilities; (3) creating economic satellites that ensure political compliance through manufactured dependency.

Tariffs
Taxes on imported goods, used to protect domestic industries or as leverage. Can be raised or lowered as an inducement or punishment. The US-China trade war (2018–present) involved escalating mutual tariffs — both states using market access as a strategic weapon.
Quotas
Limits on the quantity of imports allowed from a specific country in a given period. More targeted than tariffs — allows selective restriction of specific goods while maintaining other trade relationships. Combined with tariffs for maximum economic leverage.
Boycott
Government-organized elimination of imports from a target country — either specific commodities or the full range of that country's exports. Enforced through mandatory import licensing requirements. Historically used as collective punishment for political behavior deemed unacceptable.
Embargo
Prohibition on exports to a target country — denying it goods it needs. May cover specific strategic materials (arms, semiconductors, dual-use technology) or all goods. The US embargo on Cuba (since 1960) is one of the longest-running embargoes in history. Effectiveness depends critically on whether alternative suppliers exist.
Foreign Aid
Transfer of money, goods, or expertise from donor to recipient. Critically: most aid is not purely humanitarian. It serves donors' political, strategic, and economic objectives. Countries receive aid in proportion to their strategic importance, not their need (India, Egypt, Israel are large aid recipients for strategic reasons). Aid is routinely tied to political and economic reforms — donor countries use it to reshape recipient governments' domestic and foreign policies.
Military Aid
The oldest form of foreign assistance. Used to strengthen allies' military capabilities, reducing the need for the donor to deploy its own forces. US military aid to Ethiopia under Haile Selassie ($280 million over 23 years + training of 3,978 soldiers) enabled the emperor to suppress domestic rebellion and deter Somalia — serving American strategic interests in the Cold War Horn of Africa as much as Ethiopian security interests.
The Two Conditions for Economic Instruments to Work

Holsti argues that economic rewards and punishments only produce behavioral change in the target if two conditions are simultaneously satisfied:

① Genuine need: The target must perceive a real need for the reward (or a real threat from the punishment). If the target doesn't care about what you're offering or threatening, the instrument is useless.

② No alternative: The target must have no readily available alternative source of the good being withheld or no alternative market for the good being boycotted. If alternatives exist, the target simply reroutes — as Russia demonstrated after 2022 Western sanctions by redirecting trade to China, India, and the Gulf.

This explains why broad multilateral sanctions (involving many states simultaneously) are more effective than unilateral ones — they reduce the availability of alternatives.

Section 2.3

§ 2.3Overview of Ethiopian Foreign Policy: Six Eras

Ethiopia's foreign policy history is one of the most instructive case studies in the world for understanding how geography, ideology, and capability interact to shape a state's behavior. With over 3,000 years of statehood, more external enemies and fewer natural friends than almost any other African state, and no colonial master's legacy to follow, Ethiopia has had to construct its foreign policy from first principles in every era. What is remarkable is not how much it has changed across regimes — but how much of its fundamental strategic logic has remained constant.

The Permanent Structural Determinants of Ethiopian Foreign Policy

Geography: Ethiopia is a landlocked highland surrounded by Islamic neighbors with competing territorial claims. Located at the strategic crossroads of the Red Sea, Indian Ocean, and African interior, it has always attracted great-power interest — creating both opportunities for patronage and vulnerability to interference.

The Nile: Ethiopia controls the source of 85% of the Nile's water (Blue Nile). Egypt's existential dependence on the Nile has shaped Egypt's approach to Ethiopian politics for centuries — a structural constant regardless of which regime governs either state.

Access to the sea: Ethiopia lost its coastline when Eritrea became independent in 1993. Every regime since Menelik has grappled with the strategic and economic implications of sea access — or lack thereof. It remains unresolved.

Cultural identity: An ancient Orthodox Christian state in a predominantly Muslim neighborhood. This cultural distinctiveness has shaped alliances, threat perceptions, and the framing of foreign policy across all eras.

1855–1868
Tewodros II
Modernization, Christian Alliance, and Tragic Miscalculation

Ras Kassa — Emperor Tewodros II — inherited a fragmented empire emerging from the chaotic Era of Princes. His primary external challenge was establishing recognition of Ethiopia's sovereignty and modernity on equal terms with European powers. His central foreign policy instrument was Christianity as a diplomatic tool: he appealed specifically to Britain, France, and Russia as Christian nations to assist him against the Muslim threats of Turkey and Egypt, and to help him modernize through technology and skilled manpower.

The strategy failed catastrophically. Britain sent religious missionaries instead of the military technology Tewodros demanded; Queen Victoria refused to receive his letters as an equal sovereign. A leader who staked his legitimacy on forcing recognition was not equipped for this rebuff. His response — taking British missionaries and the consul hostage — triggered a British military expedition. His forces were easily defeated. Unwilling to surrender, Tewodros committed suicide in 1868.

Lesson: The gap between capability and aspiration is a recurring danger in Ethiopian foreign policy. Tewodros's goals were legitimate; his means were disproportionate to his power. Morgenthau's warning about prudence — matching foreign policy scope to capabilities — was illustrated in reverse.

1872–1889
Yohannes IV
Double Threat: Egypt from the North, Italy from the Coast

Yohannes IV faced a two-front strategic threat that would recur in different forms in every subsequent era: Muslim expansion (Egypt's repeated attempts to control Blue Nile sources, defeated at the battles of Gundet and Gura in 1875–76) and European colonialism (Italy acquiring the port of Massawa in 1885 — Italy's Foreign Minister framing the Red Sea as "the key to the Mediterranean").

His reign demonstrated a critical structural feature of Ethiopian geopolitics: every external actor in the Horn has interests that intersect Ethiopia's. The Sudanese Mahdists — anti-British rebels — invaded western Ethiopia because they suspected Yohannes of cooperating with Britain. Yohannes died fighting the Mahdists in 1889. Ethiopia was simultaneously managing Egyptian, Italian, Sudanese, and British vectors — a multi-front strategic burden that has never fully disappeared.

1889–1913
Menelik II
Double-Track Diplomacy and the Victory at Adwa

Menelik II was Ethiopia's most strategically sophisticated foreign policy leader before the modern era. His challenge was the classic one: a powerful colonial adversary (Italy, which now occupied Eritrea) pressing southward into Ethiopian territory, while other colonial powers (Britain, France) surrounded the country on all sides. His response was double-track diplomacy — simultaneously pursuing peaceful diplomatic solutions (treaties and agreements) while building military capability for the likely eventuality that diplomacy would fail.

The Treaty of Wuchalle (1889) with Italy appeared to offer a diplomatic settlement. But it contained a fatal ambiguity: the Amharic version made Italian mediation optional ("may use"); the Italian version made it mandatory ("shall use") — effectively placing Ethiopia under Italian protectorate. When Menelik discovered this discrepancy, he abrogated the treaty in 1893 rather than accept subordination. Italy invaded.

At the Battle of Adwa (1896), Ethiopian forces decisively defeated the Italian colonial army — the first decisive African military victory over a European colonial power in modern history. The strategic consequences were immediate: Britain, France, Russia, and Italy all came to Addis Ababa to establish formal ambassadorial relations. Ethiopia's boundaries with all its neighbors (except Sudan) were defined in formal treaties. Adwa transformed Ethiopia from a target of colonial partition into a recognized sovereign state — and a symbol of African independence that would inspire anticolonial movements for generations.

1916–1974
Haile Selassie I
The Art of Patron-Management: From the League to Washington

Haile Selassie's foreign policy was fundamentally about securing a powerful external patron to compensate for Ethiopia's limited domestic capabilities. His approach was sophisticated patron-management: always seeking to balance dependency against autonomy, never becoming so reliant on any single power that he lost all room to maneuver.

His first attempt at collective security — appealing to the League of Nations after Italy's 1935 invasion — failed spectacularly. League members, prioritizing their own interests (appeasement of Mussolini), ignored their collective security obligations. But this failure taught Haile Selassie a crucial lesson: international institutions are only as reliable as the interests of their powerful members. From that point, he focused on securing bilateral commitments from the specific power best positioned to help: first Britain (to restore his throne in 1941), then the United States (to replace the British as patron after 1952).

The 1953 US-Ethiopia agreements were a masterclass in patron-client bargaining: Ethiopia provided the US with a critical military communications base in Eritrea; the US provided security guarantees, military aid ($280 million over 23 years), and training that enabled Haile Selassie to suppress internal revolts (Eritrean nationalism, Somali irredentism, peasant uprisings) and consolidate his personal power. His foreign policy successes — founding of the OAU (1963), peacekeeping in Korea and Congo, mediation in Sudan — were real. But his abrogation of the Eritrea federation (1962) planted the seeds of 30 years of devastating civil war.

1974–1991
Military/Derg
The Soviet Patron and the Cold War Trap

The Derg's foreign policy represented a dramatic ideological rupture but a structural continuity: the same fundamental logic of patron-dependency, now dressed in socialist ideology and oriented toward Moscow instead of Washington. The Cold War's bipolarity turned the Horn of Africa into a proxy battlefield, and Ethiopia — with its strategic location and enormous military aid needs — became one of the most heavily contested prizes.

The Derg's core foreign policy objectives were: (1) regime survival; (2) territorial integrity (against Eritrean secession and Somali irredentism); (3) ideological solidarity with socialist states. When the US withdrew military assistance (1977) over human rights violations, the Soviet Union stepped in with extraordinary speed and scale — reversing its previous support for Somalia to back Ethiopia, and organizing a massive Cuban military intervention that turned the tide of the Ogaden War against Somalia. The Cold War logic was explicit: both superpowers switched regional clients based on strategic calculation, not ideology or morality.

The regime's eventual collapse was directly linked to its patron's collapse. Without Soviet military aid following Gorbachev's strategic retreat, the Derg could no longer sustain its wars against the TPLF, EPLF, and OLF simultaneously. The lesson: structural dependence on a single patron creates catastrophic vulnerability when that patron's priorities change.

Post-1991
EPRDF & Beyond
The "Inside-Out" Doctrine: Building Power from Within

The EPRDF drew a precise lesson from its predecessors' failures: patron-dependency produced not security but vulnerability. Haile Selassie fell when US support proved insufficient to suppress domestic discontent. The Derg fell when Soviet support evaporated. The pattern was clear: external support substituted for domestic legitimacy and capability, but it could always be withdrawn.

The post-1991 foreign policy doctrine inverted the traditional logic — "inside-out" rather than "outside-in." The core argument: Ethiopia's real vulnerabilities are domestic — poverty, lack of democracy, governance failures, ethnic tension. Addressing these through internal development and political reform would reduce Ethiopia's vulnerability to external threats more effectively than any military alliance or foreign patron. A prosperous, democratic, internally stable Ethiopia would generate its own strategic weight without depending on outside powers.

Economic diplomacy — attracting foreign investment, seeking export markets, accessing concessional financing — was adopted as the primary external instrument. This was framed not as dependency (like foreign aid) but as Ethiopia actively exploiting globalization's opportunities from a position of domestic strength. The "East-look" policy (learning from Asian developmental state models — Singapore, Malaysia, Indonesia) reflected the doctrine's logic: internalize best practices, build self-reliant capability, reduce conditionality-based vulnerability.

Regional leadership — through IGAD, the AU, and peacekeeping deployments (Somalia) — was pursued both as a national interest (regional stability reduces threats to Ethiopia) and as a way of building international influence disproportionate to Ethiopia's economic capabilities. Addis Ababa's role as the AU headquarters, and the FDRE's consistently active role in African diplomacy, reflect this strategy.

Changes and Continuities Across All Six Eras

Unbroken continuity: Territorial integrity has been the first-priority core value for every regime. All six governments were willing to commit maximum military and diplomatic resources to defend Ethiopia's territorial integrity. The Adwa victory remains the symbolic touchstone — the proof that this commitment can succeed.

Unbroken structural determinant: Geography. The Nile, the Red Sea, the strategic Horn location, and the pattern of Muslim-majority neighbors have shaped every regime's threat environment regardless of its ideology. As the module states: "Geography is the determining factor in the foreign policy of Ethiopia."

Change in strategy: The Imperial and Derg regimes followed an "outside-in" patron-seeking strategy. Post-1991 doctrine inverted this to "inside-out" capability-building. Whether this strategic shift proves durable — especially given the post-2018 political crisis and internal conflict — is the central question for contemporary Ethiopian foreign policy analysis.

Summary

Chapter Summary — What You Should Now Master

  • National interest is the raison d'état — the set of values, goals, and orientations a state pursues internationally. It is simultaneously a normative ideal (what should be pursued), a descriptive reality (what is actually pursued over time), and a contested political product of eight competing criteria: operational philosophy, ideological, moral/legal, pragmatic, professional advancement, partisan, ethnic/racial, and foreign dependency.
  • Realists (Morgenthau) define national interest exclusively as power and survival, demanding prudence in matching aspirations to capabilities. Idealists insist moral, legal, and ideological criteria are legitimate and necessary components of national interest, especially for addressing global collective-action problems no state can solve alone.
  • Foreign policy translates national interest into concrete goals, strategies, and instruments. Objectives are classified as short-range (core values/survival), middle-range (economic welfare/development), and long-range (systemic/ideological vision). Foreign policy behavior follows Wolfers' three patterns: self-preservation (defending the status quo), self-extension (revising it in one's favor), and self-abnegation (conceding to stronger powers).
  • Foreign policy dimensions are analyzed across alignment (alliance, neutrality, non-alignment), scope (global, regional, isolationism), and modus operandi (multilateralism vs. unilateralism).
  • Instruments of foreign policy range from diplomacy (bargaining, negotiation, summitry) through economic tools (tariffs, quotas, boycotts, embargoes, loans, foreign aid, military aid) to force. Effective economic instruments require both genuine need in the target and absence of alternatives — conditions that unilateral sanctions rarely achieve.
  • Ethiopia's foreign policy reveals remarkable structural continuity across six very different regimes: territorial integrity as the unbreakable core value; the Nile, the sea, and geography as permanent structural determinants; and a recurring struggle to convert diplomatic skill into actual capability against more powerful external actors. The post-1991 "inside-out" doctrine represents the most theoretically coherent attempt to escape the patron-dependency trap through domestic capability-building.

Diplomacy in the modern era — what historian John Lewis Gaddis called the "long peace" (the absence of great-power war since 1945) — has never been more complex or more consequential. Every challenge that defines the human future, from climate change to pandemic preparedness, nuclear proliferation to global inequality, requires the kind of structured, patient, multi-party communication that diplomacy provides. Understanding foreign policy is therefore not merely an academic exercise. It is the precondition for participating as an informed, critical citizen in the decisions your state makes about how to engage with a world that is more interconnected, and therefore more dangerous and more promising, than any previous generation has known.
